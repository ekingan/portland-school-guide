Start here

If you're new to Portland and have a child approaching school age, the system can feel overwhelming. You'll hear terms like "catchment school," "focus option," "charter," and "lottery" thrown around — often without explanation. This guide demystifies all of it so you can make an informed choice.

Portland has four distinct types of K-5 schooling: neighborhood schools, PPS focus option schools, public charter schools, and private schools. Each works differently in terms of how you enroll, who can attend, and what the educational model looks like.

The four types
Portland's School Landscape

Every school in Portland falls into one of these four categories. Understanding the differences is the first step.

🏫
Type 1

Neighborhood (Catchment) Schools

Every Portland address is assigned to a neighborhood school — also called a catchment school. This is your default option. If you don't apply to anything else, your child has a guaranteed spot at your neighborhood school.

Neighborhood schools are run directly by Portland Public Schools (PPS). They follow the district curriculum, hire through the district, and are funded by the district. Class sizes tend to be larger than alternative schools, and the educational model is more traditional — though this varies significantly by school.

The quality, culture, and community of neighborhood schools varies widely across Portland. Some have deeply engaged parent communities and outstanding teachers; others struggle with under-enrollment or aging facilities. It's worth visiting your neighborhood school before assuming it's not the right fit.

Guaranteed enrollment No application required Tuition-free Run by PPS directly Traditional curriculum
🎯
Type 2

PPS Focus Option & Language Immersion Programs

Focus option schools are operated directly by Portland Public Schools — just like neighborhood schools — but they have a specific educational focus or philosophy that sets them apart. They are open to any PPS student citywide, regardless of where you live.

Examples include Metropolitan Learning Center (MLC), which uses a progressive, student-directed model; Sunnyside Environmental K-8, which centers place-based outdoor learning; Bridger Creative Science, which integrates inquiry-based science across all subjects; and Winterhaven K-8, which focuses on STEAM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Arts, and Math) with project-based, hands-on learning.

PPS also offers Dual Language Immersion (DLI) programs — not standalone schools, but program tracks housed within existing neighborhood schools. DLI teaches the standard curriculum with instruction split between English and a partner language, starting in kindergarten and continuing through high school. PPS currently offers DLI in five languages: Chinese, Japanese, Russian, Spanish, and Vietnamese. More information at pps.net — Dual Language Immersion.

Both focus option schools and DLI programs require a centralized PPS lottery application. You can apply to up to three focus option schools and must rank them in order of preference. The lottery typically runs in late winter, with results in spring.

Note

Some PPS focus schools are also catchment schools and offer guaranteed enrollment to neighborhood families as well as a lottery-based placement.

Lottery-based admission Open to all PPS families Tuition-free Run by PPS Specialized curriculum Rank up to 3 schools
📋
Type 3

Public Charter Schools

Public charter schools are perhaps the most misunderstood category. They are fully public — tuition-free and open to any Oregon student — but they operate independently of the school district. Instead of being run by PPS, each charter school is governed by its own nonprofit board under a contract ("charter") with PPS.

This independence gives charter schools significant flexibility to design their own curriculum, hire their own teachers, and set their own school culture. This is why you'll find a wide range of models among Portland's charter schools: Waldorf (Portland Village School), Montessori (The Ivy School), project-based (Emerson School), French immersion (Le Monde), and civic/civics-focused (Cottonwood).

Funding comes primarily from Oregon's State School Fund — the same public dollars that fund PPS schools. Each charter school runs its own independent lottery; you apply separately to each one. There is no centralized charter school application.

Each school has its own lottery Open to all Oregon students Tuition-free Independently governed Publicly funded Apply to each separately
🏛️
Type 4

Private Schools

Private schools are independently operated and charge tuition. They receive no public funding and are not subject to the same oversight as public or charter schools. This gives them complete freedom over curriculum, admissions, staffing, and school culture — but it also means costs can be significant.

In Portland, notable private elementary options include Tucker Maxon School (co-enrolling deaf and hearing students since 1947), Portland Forest School (a nature-immersive K-8), and The Portland Montessori School (the city's original Montessori program, founded over 60 years ago).

Some private schools offer financial aid or sliding-scale tuition. Admission processes vary — some use applications and interviews, others have waitlists. Unlike public schools, private schools can make admissions decisions based on their own criteria.

Tuition required Independent admissions No PPS oversight Maximum curriculum flexibility Financial aid may be available
Catchment zones
What Is a Catchment School?

Your "catchment school" — or neighborhood school — is determined by your home address. Here's how it works.

Every Portland address maps to one neighborhood school

Portland Public Schools divides the city into geographic zones called catchment areas. Whichever school zone your address falls in, that's your neighborhood school. You don't have to apply — your child has a guaranteed seat there simply by virtue of where you live.

You can find your catchment school using the PPS School Finder tool: pps.net — School Finder

Can I attend a neighborhood school outside my catchment?

Yes, in some cases. If you want your child to attend a different neighborhood school — perhaps one closer to a grandparent's home or your workplace — you can submit a hardship transfer petition to PPS. This is separate from the focus option or charter lotteries. PPS reviews these on a case-by-case basis, and approval is not guaranteed.

PDX Parent has a helpful overview of the hardship petition process: pdxparent.com — Lottery Lowdown

Your neighborhood school is always your backup

No matter how the lotteries go, your catchment school will hold a spot for your child. Many families find this reassuring during an uncertain application season — there is always a place for your kid.

How enrollment works
Understanding the Lottery System

If you want to attend a focus option or charter school, you'll need to navigate one or more lotteries. Here's how they work.

There are two separate lottery systems — running at the same time

PPS Focus Option Lottery: Run centrally by Portland Public Schools. You can apply to up to three focus option schools and must rank them in order of preference. Your ranking matters — if you're offered spots at multiple schools, your higher-ranked choice takes priority. One application, submitted through PPS.

Charter School Lotteries: Each of Portland's charter schools runs its own independent lottery. You apply separately to each school, on each school's own timeline, through each school's own process. There is no single charter application.

General timeline (varies by year)

1

Fall (Oct – Nov): Research and tours

Info nights and school tours happen primarily in fall. This is the time to visit schools before application windows open.

2

Winter (Jan – Mar): Applications open

The PPS focus option application and most charter school applications open in January or February. Deadlines vary — check each school's website.

3

Spring (Mar – May): Lottery results

Charter school results typically arrive before PPS focus option results — sometimes by several weeks. Be ready to make decisions while you're still waiting on others.

4

Spring – Summer: Waitlist movement

If you're waitlisted, spots can open up as families make decisions and decline offers. Ask each school for your waitlist position number so you can gauge your chances.

5

August: Neighborhood school enrollment

If you haven't secured another placement, your neighborhood school finalizes its roster for fall. Your spot there is guaranteed right up until school starts.

If you land on a waitlist, ask your position number immediately

Schools don't always volunteer this information. A position of 5 is very different from a position of 120. Knowing your number helps you plan. It's a completely reasonable question to ask. See PDX Parent's lottery guide for more on navigating waitlists.

Key terms
Glossary

Portland school conversations are full of jargon. Here's a quick reference.

Catchment school

The neighborhood school assigned to your home address. Every Portland address has one, and enrollment is guaranteed — no application needed.

Focus option school

A PPS-operated school with a specialized theme (environmental, arts, progressive, etc.) open to all PPS students via a centralized lottery.

Charter school

A tuition-free public school that operates independently of PPS under a contract ("charter"). Has its own board, curriculum, and lottery process.

Lottery

A random selection process used by focus option and charter schools when there are more applicants than open spots. Usually run once per year in spring.

Waitlist

If a school is full, you may be placed on a waitlist. Spots open up as families decline offers. Ask for your position number — it matters.

Hardship petition

A request submitted to PPS to attend a neighborhood school outside your catchment. Approved on a case-by-case basis for reasons like proximity to childcare or a parent's workplace.

PPS

Portland Public Schools — the public school district that serves Portland. Operates neighborhood schools and focus option schools, and sponsors (but does not run) charter schools.

Montessori / Waldorf / Project-based

Alternative education philosophies. Montessori emphasizes self-directed learning; Waldorf integrates arts; project-based learning uses real-world inquiry. Several Portland charters and privates use these models.
