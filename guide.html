How the system works

Navigating the Lotteries

Portland's school application system has multiple tracks running at the same time — each with its own rules, calendar, and quirks. Here's what we learned firsthand.

📖 Required reading: PDX Parent's lottery guide

PDX Parent has written the definitive guide to this process. It covers how the PPS focus option lottery works, how to apply to charter schools, how to submit a hardship petition to attend a neighborhood school outside your catchment, and what to do if you land on a waitlist. We referred back to it throughout our entire process.

Read "Everything You Need to Know About the PPS School Lottery" → pdxparent.com

The two parallel systems

PPS Focus Option Lottery

Run centrally by Portland Public Schools. You can select up to three focus option schools (MLC, Bridger Creative Science, Sunnyside Environmental, and others) and must rank them in order of preference. Your ranking matters — a higher-ranked school takes priority if you're offered a seat in more than one. Results typically arrive several weeks after the lottery window closes.

Charter School Lotteries

Each of Portland's seven charter schools (Cottonwood, Emerson, The Ivy School, Kairos PDX, Le Monde, Portland Arthur Academy, Portland Village School) runs its own independent lottery. You apply separately to each one. Deadlines and processes vary by school — check each school's website directly.

Timing: charters first, PPS second

Both lotteries run roughly in the same window, but they don't land at exactly the same time. In our experience, charter school results came in before the PPS focus option results. This means you may be making decisions about waitlists — or accepting offers — before you've heard from all the schools you applied to. Build in some mental flexibility for that overlap.

⚡ Waitlists: ask your position immediately

If you land on a waitlist, contact the school right away and ask your specific position number. This is critical information that schools don't always volunteer upfront. A waitlist position of 5 is very different from 120 — and knowing your number helps you decide whether to hold other offers or move on.

From our own experience: we were waitlisted at position 120 at Le Monde (effectively out of reach), and at position 30 at both Emerson and The Ivy School. Knowing those numbers helped us stay patient and focused on the right schools.

Be ready to move fast

When a spot opens off a waitlist, schools need an answer quickly — sometimes within 24–48 hours. We got the call from Emerson unexpectedly and had to make a fast decision. Know in advance how you'd rank your waitlisted schools if multiple spots opened at once, so you're not making a high-stakes choice under pressure with no preparation.

The hardship petition: attending a non-catchment neighborhood school

If you want to send your child to a neighborhood school outside your assigned catchment — for example, because of proximity to a grandparent's home, a sibling's school, or a work commute — you can submit a hardship petition to PPS. This is separate from the focus option lottery. PDX Parent's article above covers how to submit one and what qualifies. It's worth knowing this option exists even if you don't end up needing it.

Your neighborhood school is your guaranteed fallback

No matter how the lotteries go, your assigned neighborhood school will hold a spot for your child. We kept this in mind throughout the process — it made the uncertainty of waitlists much easier to sit with, knowing there was always a place for our kid regardless of outcome.

A concern that shaped our search

Earthquake Safety

This section reflects our personal experience and values. Seismic safety was a major factor in our school search — but we recognize it may not carry the same weight for every family. We're sharing what we learned in case it's useful.

Why this mattered to us

We have personal experience with a major earthquake. That experience made seismic risk feel viscerally real in a way it might not for families who haven't lived through one. It became a lens through which we evaluated every school building — and ultimately steered us away from a number of Portland Public Schools we might otherwise have considered seriously.

Portland's seismic risk is real and well-documented

Portland sits near the Cascadia Subduction Zone, a major offshore fault capable of producing a magnitude 8–9 earthquake. Oregon emergency management estimates a roughly 37% chance of a major Cascadia event in the next 50 years. In such a quake, buildings made of unreinforced masonry — older brick and concrete block construction — are at the highest risk of collapse. Many of Portland's school buildings are old, and some are built exactly this way.

⚠️ 19 PPS schools are in unreinforced masonry buildings

A 2024 seismic assessment commissioned by Portland Public Schools and conducted by Holmes Consulting Group identified 19 elementary and middle school buildings across the district made of unreinforced masonry (URM) — the construction type most likely to collapse in even a moderate earthquake. Retrofitting just the URM buildings alone is estimated to cost over $118 million; addressing seismic deficiencies across all PPS schools is estimated at roughly $900 million.

Source: Willamette Week, May 2025

The 2025 bond and seismic commitments

In May 2025, Portland voters approved a $1.83 billion school bond. Prior to the vote, parent advocates pushed the School Board to formally commit a portion of the funds to seismic upgrades. The Board passed Resolution 7109, directing funds toward the highest-risk buildings first using a transparent, data-driven prioritization process. Some retrofits are already underway or scheduled. However, the full scope of work across all PPS schools remains far larger than current funding can cover, and the sequencing of which schools get upgraded — and when — is still unfolding.

PPS's official seismic assessment page: pps.net — Seismic Assessments
Retrofit progress tracker: bond.pps.net — Seismic Improvements

Schools that have already been retrofitted

Some PPS schools have received seismic upgrades through earlier bond cycles. Per Willamette Week's reporting, Franklin, Grant, and Roosevelt high schools have been fully retrofitted, along with Alameda, Lewis, and Kellogg primary schools. Of the neighborhood schools we visited, Alameda's retrofit history was one factor that kept it on our list longer than others. Building age alone isn't a perfect guide — it matters what the building is made of and whether retrofit work has been done.

How we thought about charter and private schools

Several of the schools we were most drawn to are housed in newer buildings or smaller structures that don't carry the same URM risk profile as the older PPS brick schoolhouses. The Emerson School's "forever home" is a modern building. The Portland Montessori School's campus was in a building built in 1940 and was mostly constructed of wood. For any school, we'd recommend asking directly: what year was this building constructed, is it on the City of Portland's URM list, and has any seismic work been done?

Questions to ask on a school tour

Is this building on Portland's Unreinforced Masonry (URM) list?

The City of Portland maintains a public list of URM buildings. Schools can confirm this directly.

Has this building received seismic retrofitting, and when?

Ask for specifics — partial retrofits and full retrofits are meaningfully different.

What is the school's earthquake emergency plan?

Drop, cover, hold on — and then what? Ask how the school communicates with families after an event and where the reunification site is.

For PPS schools: what is this building's seismic risk score?

The Holmes Consulting Group assessment assigned risk scores to all PPS buildings. The full report is public — you can look up your school before or after a tour.

How it unfolded

We were accepted to Portland Village School and MLC, but ultimately didn't move forward with either. Portland Village School felt like too far a commute for our family, and we had reservations about the brick structure that houses MLC — a significant concern given our focus on earthquake safety. While we waited on our charter waitlists, we thought our most likely path was The Portland Montessori School. Then the call came from Emerson, and the decision was easy.

What we prioritized
🌍

Diverse student body

We wanted our child in a classroom that reflects the full range of Portland's community — racially, economically, and culturally.

🧠

Child-centered learning

Alternative models — Montessori, project-based, Waldorf — that follow the child's curiosity.

🌿

Outdoor time

Daily recess and nature connection, not just a 15-minute break between desks.

🚲

Bikeability

Could we realistically bike there most days? Location and safe routes mattered.

🤝

Parent community

Engaged families who show up — for volunteering, events, and real relationships.

🏗️

Earthquake safety

Portland sits on a major fault. We looked at building age and seismic upgrade status.

Why Emerson rose to the top

The project-based approach keeps kids engaged with real-world questions. They get daily movement outside in addition to recess. The school runs weekly 5th-grade book buddies and all-school mixed-grade activities that build genuine belonging. And their new "forever home" in SW Portland is a modern, light-filled space that passed our building safety check. The cherry on top: it's tuition-free as a public charter.

Why Portland Montessori was a close second

Oregon's oldest Montessori program — over 60 years running — on a one-acre campus in NE Portland. The Montessori method aligned perfectly with our values around self-directed learning and mixed-age classrooms. The school is now independently governed with an active parent advisory committee. The key tradeoff: it's private with tuition, and priority goes to kids with prior Montessori experience.

What We'd Tell Other Portland Parents

Lessons Learned

Things we wish someone had told us before we started this process.

1

Visit the schools in person — it's non-negotiable

No website, virtual tour, or GreatSchools rating tells you what 20 minutes on campus will. You'll feel the energy immediately. Watch how adults talk to children. Pay attention to how kids interact with each other at recess. Ask teachers why they chose this school — their answers are revealing.

2

Align with your co-parent before you start touring

It sounds obvious, but agreeing on your top two or three criteria before you set foot in any school will save you a lot of post-visit arguments. What matters most — curriculum philosophy? Proximity? Diversity? Cost? Get aligned early so you're evaluating the same things.

3

Apply broadly — even to schools you're only "kind of" interested in

Waitlists move. Lottery results are unpredictable. Apply to anything you'd genuinely say yes to if the call came. You can always decline. The cost of applying is low; the cost of not applying when a spot opens is high.

4

Understand each application timeline — they're all different

PPS focus options, public charters, and private schools all run on different calendars with different deadlines. Charter schools have their own independent lotteries. Miss a window and you might wait a full year. Start a spreadsheet in October.

5

The neighborhood school option is underrated

Don't dismiss your neighborhood school before visiting. The convenience and community of walking to school with your neighbors has real value that's easy to underestimate on paper. Some of the warmest school visits we had were at neighborhood schools with deeply engaged parent communities.

6

Ask about the building

Portland sits near the Cascadia Subduction Zone. Seismic safety is a real consideration. Ask specifically whether the building has been seismically upgraded. Newer buildings are generally safer; some older school buildings in Portland have not yet been retrofitted. This is public information — don't be shy about asking.

Background

About This Guide

A quick note on who we are and how we approached this process.

Who we are

We're a Portland family who went through kindergarten school selection and wanted to share what we found with other families doing the same research. We have no affiliation with any of the schools listed. Our child now attends The Emerson School, and we couldn't be happier — but every family's priorities are different, and the right school for your kid might look very different from ours.

How we ran our process

Tours (Nov – April)

We visited or attended info nights at all of our neighborhood schools, private schools, and some of the charter schools. It was hard to get to all of them but it was the most helpful part of our process.

Applications (Jan – March)

We applied to every school we'd possibly say yes to. PPS focus options, charter lotteries, and private schools all had different deadlines and processes.

Waiting (April – June)

Lottery results came in waves. We ranked our waitlists and held our neighborhood school spot as a backup until we had a firm offer we felt good about.

Decision

Emerson came off the waitlist and we made a fast pivot. The staff let us come by the school one more time to be sure. It was the easiest call we made in the whole process.
